## 🏛️ Concept Primer: Juvenile Justice & Defamation

In this module, we analyze high-yield legal principles extracted from recent CLAT papers, focusing on the **Juvenile Justice Act, 2015** and the evolving standards of **Online Defamation**.

### 1. The Juvenile Justice Framework
The Indian legal system emphasizes the welfare of children. The **Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act, 2015** is the primary legislation. 
- **Core Principle**: Rehabilitation over punishment.
- **Critical Clause**: Children between the ages of **16 and 18** can be tried as adults only after a **preliminary assessment** by the Juvenile Justice Board (JJB) for heinous offenses.

### 2. Online Defamation & Free Speech
Balancing the **Right to Freedom of Expression** (Article 19) with the **Right to Reputation** is a recurring theme in legal reasoning.
- **Proof Requirement**: To succeed in a defamation claim, the plaintiff must prove the statement was:
    1. False
    2. Damaging to their reputation
    3. Made with a degree of fault (negligence or malice).

## 📖 Reading Passage X: Juvenile Justice Act, 2015

*"The Indian legal system places a significant emphasis on protecting the rights and welfare of children. The Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act, 2015, is a vital piece of legislation in this regard. It is designed to ensure that children in conflict with the law receive special care, protection, and treatment, with the ultimate goal of their rehabilitation and reintegration into society."*

### 🔍 Trap Analysis
- **Trap**: "Children under 18 are *never* tried as adults." 
- **Fact**: The JJB can assess 16-18 year olds for heinous crimes. Always look for the "Preliminary Assessment" keyword in the options.

## 📖 Reading Passage XI: Online Defamation

*"Online defamation cases often involve a balancing act between the right to freedom of expression and the right to reputation. The Indian legal system requires a careful examination of the content, context, and intent of statements to determine whether they qualify as defamatory. Additionally, the plaintiff in an online defamation case must prove that the statement was false, damaging to their reputation, and made with a degree of fault, such as negligence or actual malice."*

### 🔍 Trap Analysis
- **Trap**: "Freedom of expression is absolute in the digital space."
- **Fact**: It is subject to reasonable restrictions, including the protection of an individual's reputation.
