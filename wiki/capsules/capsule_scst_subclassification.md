## The Problem: Why This Case Happened

**Main Contextual Influence**: Decades of administrative data revealed that within the Scheduled Castes umbrella, a few relatively advanced sub-castes were consuming the vast majority of reservation benefits, completely shutting out more historically degraded sub-groups (e.g., Valmikis and Mazhabi Sikhs in Punjab, Madigas in Andhra Pradesh).

---

## Core Legal Underpinnings

### 1. Overruling Precedent

This judgment explicitly **overruled** the 5-judge bench decision in **E.V. Chinnaiah v. State of Andhra Pradesh (2004)**, which had erroneously held that the SC list under Article 341 forms a single, monolithic, homogeneous class that states cannot alter.

> **CLAT Strategy**: If a passage presents an argument based on *Chinnaiah*, it is the **wrong** argument. The correct answer must acknowledge that *Chinnaiah* was overruled.

### 2. Article 14 & 16(4) Interplay

The majority ruled that the principle of **"Sub-classification"** is a facet of **substantive equality**. True equality requires treating unequals unequally within a class to prevent the exclusion of the weakest.

**The Critical Rider**: States cannot reserve **100% of a quota** for a single sub-class and must base sub-classification on **quantifiable, empirical data of under-representation**.

| Principle | Formal Equality | Substantive Equality |
|-----------|----------------|---------------------|
| **Definition** | Same treatment for everyone | Different treatment based on actual disadvantage |
| **Source** | *Chinnaiah* (2004) | *Davinder Singh* (2024) |
| **Application** | SC list = one homogeneous class | SC list = heterogeneous; sub-classification permitted |
| **Test** | Article 14 = sameness | Article 14 + 16(4) = differential treatment to achieve real equality |

## CLAT Passage Strategy

### The Three Things You Must Know

1. **The exact trigger** (August 1, 2024, 7-judge bench, 6:1 majority)
2. **The constitutional/legal provision at stake** (Art. 14, Art. 16(4), Art. 341)
3. **The competing interest that was overridden and why** (formal equality → substantive equality; *Chinnaiah* overruled because monolithic classification excluded the weakest sub-groups)

### How CLAT Will Test This

The key doctrinal distinction is between **formal equality** (same treatment for all) and **substantive equality** (different treatment based on actual disadvantage).

- The passage will likely present a scenario where a sub-group within SC/ST claims under-representation, and the state wants to create a sub-quota.
- The correct answer will **ALWAYS** reference the need for **quantifiable, empirical data** — not just historical grievance or political demand.
- **Remember**: *E.V. Chinnaiah* was **overruled**. Do not select an answer based on *Chinnaiah*.
- The **100% rider** is crucial: a state cannot earmark the entire quota for one sub-class. Some portion must remain open to all SCs.

### Trap Answers to Avoid

- ❌ "The SC list is a monolithic class" → *Chinnaiah* was overruled
- ❌ "States can sub-classify without any data" → empirical data is required
- ❌ "A state can reserve 100% of the SC quota for one sub-class" → the Court placed a rider against this
- ✅ "Sub-classification is a facet of substantive equality under Articles 14 and 16(4)" → CORRECT
