## 1. Negligence (Most Tested Tort)

### The Neighbour Principle (Donoghue v. Stevenson, 1932)

**Rule**: You must take reasonable care to avoid acts or omissions which you can reasonably foresee would likely injure your **neighbour** — someone so closely and directly affected by your act that you ought reasonably to have them in contemplation.

**The Four Elements of Negligence** (ALL must be present):

| Element | Definition | CLAT Test |
|---------|------------|-----------|
| **1. Duty of Care** | The defendant owed a duty to the plaintiff to avoid foreseeable harm | Would a reasonable person foresee harm to the plaintiff? |
| **2. Breach** | The defendant fell below the standard of a "reasonable person" | Did the defendant act less carefully than a reasonable person would? |
| **3. Causation** | The breach caused the harm (causa causans) | Would the harm have occurred "but for" the defendant's act? |
| **4. Damage** | The plaintiff actually suffered loss or injury | Was there actual harm — physical, financial, or reputational? |

### Res Ipsa Loquitur ("The Thing Speaks for Itself")

**When it applies**:
1. The accident is of a kind that **ordinarily does not happen without negligence**.
2. The instrumentality causing the harm was under the defendant's **exclusive control**.
3. The plaintiff did NOT contribute to the accident.

**Effect**: The **burden of proof shifts** to the defendant to prove they were NOT negligent.

**CLAT Example**:
> "A patient undergoes surgery. After the operation, a surgical instrument is found inside the patient's body. The hospital claims it was an unavoidable accident."

**Analysis**:
- Surgical instruments left inside patients do NOT ordinarily happen without negligence.
- The operating room was under the hospital's exclusive control.
- **Res Ipsa Loquitur applies** → burden shifts to the hospital to prove it was NOT negligent.

> **CLAT Trap**: Res Ipsa does NOT mean the defendant is automatically liable. It only shifts the burden. The defendant can still prove they were not negligent.

### Contributory Negligence

**Rule**: If the plaintiff's own carelessness contributed to their injury, damages are **reduced proportionally**.

**CLAT Example**:
> "A pedestrian jaywalks while looking at their phone. A speeding driver hits them. Both are at fault."

**Analysis**:
- Driver breached duty of care (speeding).
- Pedestrian was contributorily negligent (jaywalking + distraction).
- **Damages reduced proportionally** based on each party's fault.

## 2. Defamation (Libel & Slander)

### Essentials of Defamation (ALL required):

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| **1. Publication** | The statement must be communicated to at least one third party |
| **2. Reference to Plaintiff** | The statement must refer to the plaintiff (directly or indirectly) |
| **3. Defamatory Nature** | The statement must lower the plaintiff's reputation in the eyes of a reasonable person |
| **4. Falsity** | The statement must be false (truth is a complete defence) |

### Types of Defamation

| Type | Medium | Example | CLAT Note |
|------|--------|---------|-----------|
| **Libel** | Permanent/written form | Newspaper article, social media post, blog | More serious; actionable per se |
| **Slander** | Transient/spoken form | False spoken accusation in public | Requires proof of special damage (usually) |

### Defences to Defamation

| Defence | When It Applies | CLAT Example |
|---------|-----------------|--------------|
| **Justification (Truth)** | The statement is substantially true | "He called me a thief. But I was convicted of theft." → Defence succeeds |
| **Fair Comment** | Honest opinion on matter of public interest | "His acting was terrible" — opinion, not fact |
| **Absolute Privilege** | Statements made in judicial/parliamentary proceedings | A witness testifying in court cannot be sued for defamation |
| **Qualified Privilege** | Statements made in good faith without malice | Employer giving reference letter; police reporting crime |

> **CLAT Trap**: A statement that is TRUE but made with malice may still be defamatory if it lacks qualified privilege. However, TRUTH is an absolute defence to libel.

## 3. Strict Liability vs. Absolute Liability

### Strict Liability (Rylands v. Fletcher, 1868)

**Rule**: A person who brings onto their land and collects something that is **likely to do mischief if it escapes** is strictly liable for any damage caused by its escape.

**Requirements**:
1. The defendant brought something onto their land.
2. The thing is **non-natural use** of the land (not ordinary).
3. The thing **escaped** from the defendant's land.
4. The escape caused **damage**.

**Exceptions** (Where Strict Liability Does NOT Apply):
- **Act of God** (natural disaster beyond human control)
- **Plaintiff's own fault** (the plaintiff caused the escape)
- **Third party's act** (someone else caused the escape without defendant's negligence)
- **Consent** (the plaintiff consented to the risk)

### Absolute Liability (M.C. Mehta v. Union of India, 1987)

**Rule**: For **hazardous or inherently dangerous industries**, the enterprise is **absolutely liable** for ANY harm caused — NO exceptions.

**The "Oleum Gas Leak" Principle**:
- After the Bhopal Gas Tragedy (1984), the Supreme Court in *M.C. Mehta* (1987) established that industries dealing with hazardous substances cannot escape liability.
- **No exceptions** — not even act of God or third-party fault.
- The enterprise must pay **compensatory damages** regardless of fault.

**CLAT Comparison**:

| | Strict Liability | Absolute Liability |
|--|------------------|--------------------|
| **Source** | *Rylands v. Fletcher* (1868) | *M.C. Mehta v. UOI* (1987) |
| **Scope** | Non-natural use of land | Hazardous/dangerous industries |
| **Exceptions** | Act of God, plaintiff's fault, third party, consent | **NONE** |
| **Fault required?** | No — liability without proof of negligence | No |
| **Indian context** | General rule | Hazardous industries (chemical, nuclear, etc.) |

**CLAT Example**:
> "A chemical factory stores toxic waste. An earthquake causes the waste to leak into a nearby river, killing fish and poisoning water. The factory claims it is not liable because the earthquake was an act of God."

**Analysis**:
- Is the factory a hazardous industry? **YES** (chemical factory).
- Does Absolute Liability apply? **YES** (*M.C. Mehta*).
- Can the factory claim "act of God"? **NO** — absolute liability has NO exceptions.
- **Conclusion**: The factory is LIABLE.

> **CLAT Strategy**: If the passage involves a **hazardous industry**, the answer is almost always **Absolute Liability** (*M.C. Mehta*), not Strict Liability (*Rylands*). No exceptions apply.

## 4. Vicarious Liability

**Rule**: One person (master/employer) is held liable for torts committed by another (servant/agent) **in the course of employment**.

**Key Distinction**: Was the tort committed **in the course of employment** or during a **personal frolic**?

| Scenario | Course of Employment? | Vicarious Liability? |
|----------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Delivery driver hits someone while delivering packages | **YES** | **YES** |
| Delivery driver hits someone while on a personal weekend trip | **NO** (personal frolic) | **NO** |
| Taxi driver hits someone while driving a passenger | **YES** | **YES** |
| Taxi driver hits someone while driving home after shift | **NO** | **NO** |
| Employee injures someone while performing a task authorized by employer | **YES** | **YES** |
| Employee injures someone while doing something completely unrelated to work | **NO** | **NO** |

> **CLAT Trap**: The phrase "in the course of employment" is the KEY. Look for whether the employee was acting within the scope of their duties when the tort occurred.

## 5. General Defences in Tort

| Defence | Rule | CLAT Example |
|---------|------|--------------|
| **Volenti Non Fit Injuria** | No injury to one who consents | A boxer injured during a match cannot sue the opponent |
| **Act of God** | Natural disaster beyond human control | Flood destroys crops — no liability if unforeseeable |
| **Inevitable Accident** | Could not be prevented despite reasonable care | Sudden heart attack while driving |
| **Plaintiff the Wrongdoer** | Cannot recover if plaintiff was also at fault | Thief injured while trespassing |
| **Statutory Authority** | Act authorized by law | Government demolishing unsafe building under statute |
| **Private Defence** | Reasonable force to protect person/property | See Criminal Law section |

> **Critical Distinction for Volenti**: Consent must be **free and informed**. A worker forced to work in unsafe conditions has NOT consented.
