## The Problem: Why This Case Happened

**Main Contextual Influence**: Introduced in the 2018 Finance Act, the scheme allowed unlimited, completely anonymous political donations by corporations. This created a massive asymmetry of information where the ruling party could access donor identities via state-owned banks (SBI), while the voting public remained entirely blind to potential quid pro quo corruption.

---

## Core Legal Underpinnings

### 1. The Four-Pronged Proportionality Test

The Supreme Court applied the strict **four-pronged Proportionality Test**:

| Prong | Test | Result |
|-------|------|--------|
| **1. Legitimate Aim** | Does the measure pursue a legitimate state aim? | ✓ Curbing black money |
| **2. Rational Connection** | Is there a rational connection between the measure and the aim? | ✓ Yes |
| **3. Least Restrictive Means** | Is the measure the least restrictive means available? | ✗ **NO** — total anonymity is not the least restrictive means |
| **4. Proportionality Stricto Sensu** | Does the measure strike a fair balance? | ✗ The harm to voter information outweighs the benefit |

> **CLAT Strategy**: The scheme failed at **Prong 3** (least restrictive means). The Court held that total donor anonymity was **not necessary** to curb black money. Less restrictive alternatives exist (e.g., partial disclosure, capped anonymity, real-time reporting).

### 2. Infringement of Article 19(1)(a)

The court established that the **Right to Information** is a vital structural subset of **Freedom of Expression**. For a voter to exercise their franchise effectively, information regarding who funds political parties is a constitutional prerequisite.

> **Key Doctrine**: Art. 19(1)(a) → Right to Information → Informed Voting

## CLAT Passage Strategy

### The Three Things You Must Know

1. **The exact trigger** (February 15, 2024, anonymous 5-judge bench, unanimous)
2. **The constitutional/legal provision at stake** (Art. 19(1)(a) — freedom of speech and expression, including right to information)
3. **The competing interest that was overridden and why** (donor privacy → voter's right to know; total anonymity was not the least restrictive means)

### How CLAT Will Test This

- The passage will likely present a balance between two rights: (1) the **right to privacy of donors** vs. (2) the **right to information of voters** under Article 19(1)(a).
- The correct answer will **favor the voter's right to know** — because the Court held that information about political funding is essential for informed voting.
- The Court ordered **SBI to disclose donor data**. This became a major post-verdict controversy.
- If the question asks "what is the least restrictive means to curb black money?" the answer will be about **transparency mechanisms** (e.g., real-time disclosure, capped donations), not blanket anonymity.

### The Proportionality Test Structure (Memorize)

When you see a proportionality question in CLAT, apply this exact framework:

```
1. Legitimate Aim?     → Is there a valid state goal?
2. Rational Connection? → Does the measure actually advance that goal?
3. Least Restrictive?  → Is there a less harmful way to achieve the same goal?
4. Fair Balance?       → Does the benefit outweigh the harm?
```

The Electoral Bonds scheme failed at **Prong 3**.
