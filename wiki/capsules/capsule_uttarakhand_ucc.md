# 💊 Study Capsule: Uttarakhand Uniform Civil Code (UCC)

> **CLAT Relevance**: ★★★★☆ (Very High Yield) | **Time to Master**: 20 minutes | **Passage Type**: Constitutional Law + DPSP vs. Fundamental Rights

---

## The Exact Trigger (Memorize Cold)

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| **February 7, 2024** | Passed by Uttarakhand Legislative Assembly |
| **March 11, 2024** | Received **Presidential Assent under Article 254(2)** |

> ⚠️ **CLAT Trap**: Article 254(2) Presidential Assent is **significant** — it is what allows a **state law** to **override central personal laws** on the Concurrent List. Examiners may test whether you understand why that assent was necessary.

---

## The Problem: Why This Code Happened

**Main Contextual Influence**: The political push to fulfill **Article 44** of the Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSPs), which urges the State to secure a uniform civil code for all citizens. The historical friction lay in balancing secular gender-equal laws with religious autonomy guaranteed under **Articles 25 and 26**.

---

## Core Legal Underpinnings

### 1. The Tribal Conundrum: Is It Truly "Uniform"?

To protect customs protected under **Part XXI** of the Constitution, the Code explicitly **exempts Scheduled Tribes** (under Article 366(25)). This created an immediate constitutional critique regarding whether a code can truly be "uniform" if it excludes specific demographic segments based on ethnicity.

| Argument | Response |
|----------|----------|
| "A code must be uniform to satisfy Article 44" | Article 44 is a **DPSP** — aspirational, not enforceable. The Constitution itself permits exceptions (Part XXI, Article 366(25)). |
| "Excluding STs violates equality" | The exemption is to protect **customary rights** recognized by the Constitution. This is a **reasonable classification** under Article 14. |

> **CLAT Strategy**: The tension is **Article 44 (DPSP — aspirational)** vs. **Part XXI protections (binding)**. The correct answer should acknowledge that while imperfect, the Code is still a significant step toward Article 44, and the ST exemption is constitutionally justified under Article 366(25).

### 2. Privacy vs. State Regulation: The Live-In Relationship Clause

By **criminalizing the non-registration of live-in relationships** with up to **3 months of imprisonment**, the law directly collided with the right to autonomy and spatial privacy established in **K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017)**.

| Right | Conflict | CLAT Angle |
|-------|----------|------------|
| **Article 21** — Right to Privacy (Puttaswamy) | State wants to regulate live-in relationships | Is criminalization proportionate? |
| **Article 44** — UCC (DPSP) | State wants uniform civil law | Can DPSP override FR? |
| **Articles 25 & 26** — Religious freedom | Code overrides personal laws | Does Article 254(2) assent validate this? |

> **CLAT Strategy**: Expect a passage that asks whether criminalizing non-registration is **constitutionally proportionate**. The answer should reference the **proportionality test** from *Puttaswamy*: the state must show that criminalization is necessary, proportionate, and the least restrictive means. Given that registration is an administrative requirement, **criminalization** (up to 3 months imprisonment) may be seen as disproportionate.

## CLAT Passage Strategy

### The Three Things You Must Know

1. **The exact trigger** (Feb 7, 2024 passed; March 11, 2024 Presidential Assent under Art. 254(2))
2. **The constitutional/legal provision at stake** (Art. 44 DPSP, Art. 254(2), Art. 366(25), Art. 21 — Puttaswamy privacy)
3. **The competing interest that was overridden and why** (religious autonomy under Arts. 25/26 → gender equality under Art. 44; privacy under Art. 21 → state regulation of live-in relationships)

### How CLAT Will Test This

- The passage will likely present a conflict between **Article 44 (DPSP — UCC)** and **Articles 25/26 (religious freedom)**, or **Article 21 (privacy)**.
- The correct answer will often reference the **Shah Bano** principle: **gender equality is a constitutional imperative**, and personal laws cannot override it.
- The UCC's criminalization of live-in relationships is a high-yield topic for questions on **privacy vs. state regulation**.
- The fact that STs are exempt raises questions about whether the code is truly "uniform" — this is a classic **trap answer**. The correct answer should acknowledge that while imperfect, it is still a significant step toward Article 44, and the ST exemption is constitutionally justified.

### Pressure Points for Passages

1. **ST Exemption**: Is a "uniform" code constitutionally coherent if it excludes STs? The tension is **Article 44 (DPSP — aspirational)** vs. **Part XXI protections (binding)**.
2. **Live-In Criminalization**: This is a direct **fundamental rights conflict** — expect a passage that asks whether criminalizing non-registration is constitutionally proportionate under the **Puttaswamy** framework.
