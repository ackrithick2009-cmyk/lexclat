## 1. Syllogisms — The Venn Diagram Method

### Basic Structure

A syllogism has:
- **Two premises** (statements)
- **One conclusion**
- You must determine whether the conclusion **DEFINITELY FOLLOWS** from the premises.

### The Four Standard Forms (Memorize)

| Form | Statement | Diagram |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **All A are B** | Universal Affirmative | A circle completely inside B |
| **No A are B** | Universal Negative | A and B circles do NOT overlap |
| **Some A are B** | Particular Affirmative | A and B circles overlap partially |
| **Some A are not B** | Particular Negative | Part of A is outside B |

### The Venn Diagram Approach

**Step 1**: Draw the first premise.
**Step 2**: Draw the second premise, overlapping or intersecting as needed.
**Step 3**: Check if the conclusion is true in **ALL possible valid diagrams**.

> **Key Rule**: A conclusion is valid only if it is true in **EVERY possible diagram** that satisfies the premises. If you can draw even ONE valid diagram where the conclusion is false, the conclusion does NOT follow.

### Worked Example

**Premise 1**: All cats are animals.  
**Premise 2**: Some animals are dogs.  
**Conclusion**: Some cats are dogs.

**Venn Diagram Analysis**:
- Draw a circle for "cats" completely inside "animals" (Premise 1).
- Draw "dogs" overlapping with "animals" (Premise 2).
- **Possibility 1**: "dogs" overlaps with "cats" → Some cats are dogs. ✓
- **Possibility 2**: "dogs" overlaps only with the non-cat part of "animals" → No cats are dogs. ✗

**Conclusion**: Since Possibility 2 is valid, the conclusion "Some cats are dogs" does NOT definitely follow.

> **CLAT Trap**: The conclusion "Some cats are dogs" SOUNDS true because some animals are dogs and all cats are animals. But the conclusion must follow in ALL valid diagrams. If even one valid diagram contradicts it, the conclusion is invalid.

### Common Valid Conclusions (Memorize)

| Premise 1 | Premise 2 | Valid Conclusion |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| All A are B | All B are C | **All A are C** |
| All A are B | Some B are C | **Some A are C** (if A and C overlap through B) |
| No A are B | All B are C | **No A are C** (if C = B) — but depends on diagram |
| Some A are B | All B are C | **Some A are C** |

### Invalid Conclusions (Traps)

| Premise 1 | Premise 2 | Invalid Conclusion | Why? |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|------|
| All A are B | Some B are C | All A are C | A and C may not overlap |
| Some A are B | Some B are C | Some A are C | No necessary connection between A and C |
| No A are B | Some B are C | No A are C | C may include A through a different path |

## 2. Seating Arrangements — Linear & Circular

### Linear Arrangements

**Structure**: People sitting in a row, facing north or south.

**Key Rules**:
- "To the left of" and "to the right of" depend on facing direction.
- If facing **north**, left is your left.
- If facing **south**, left is your right (from the viewer's perspective).

**Example**:
> "6 people sit in a row facing north. A is third from the left. B is to the immediate right of A. C is second from the right."

**Approach**:
1. Draw 6 blank positions: _ _ _ _ _ _
2. Place A at position 3: _ _ A _ _ _
3. Place B immediately to A's right: _ _ A B _ _
4. C is second from the right (position 5): _ _ A B C _
5. Fill remaining positions with other information.

### Circular Arrangements

**Structure**: People sitting around a table, facing center or outward.

**Key Rules**:
- If facing **center**, left and right are from the person's perspective.
- "Immediate left" means the person directly next to you on your left.
- "Opposite" means directly across the table (for even number of people).

**Example**:
> "8 people sit around a circular table facing the center. A sits opposite B. C sits to the immediate left of A. D sits second to the right of B."

**Approach**:
1. Draw a circle with 8 positions.
2. Place A and B opposite each other (4 positions apart).
3. Place C immediately to A's left.
4. Place D second to B's right (skip one position).
5. Fill remaining positions.

> **CLAT Strategy**: Seating arrangements are PURE LOGIC. Don't overthink. Draw the diagram, place the fixed positions first, and use elimination. If you can't place someone with certainty, note the possibilities and come back.

## 3. Blood Relations — Family Tree Logic

### Key Relationships (Memorize)

| Relationship | Meaning | Example |
|-------------|---------|---------|
| **Father's son** | Brother or self (if male) | "My father's son" = my brother or me |
| **Mother's daughter** | Sister or self (if female) | "My mother's daughter" = my sister or me |
| **Father's brother** | Paternal uncle | "My father's brother" = my uncle |
| **Mother's sister** | Maternal aunt | "My mother's sister" = my aunt |
| **Sister's son** | Nephew | "My sister's son" = my nephew |
| **Brother's daughter** | Niece | "My brother's daughter" = my niece |
| **Father's father** | Grandfather | "My father's father" = my grandfather |
| **Mother's mother** | Grandmother | "My mother's mother" = my grandmother |
| **Son of my son** | Grandson | "My son's son" = my grandson |
| **Daughter of my daughter** | Granddaughter | "My daughter's daughter" = my granddaughter |

### The Generational Approach

**Step 1**: Identify the **generations** in the statement.
**Step 2**: Draw a **family tree** with males (□) and females (○).
**Step 3**: Use the relationship to connect people across generations.

**Example**:
> "Pointing to a man, a woman said, 'His mother is the only daughter of my mother.' How is the woman related to the man?"

**Analysis**:
- "Only daughter of my mother" = the woman herself (since she is the only daughter).
- So, "His mother is ME."
- Therefore, the woman is the man's **mother**.

> **CLAT Strategy**: Blood relations are about **decoding generational language**. Break the statement into pieces, identify who "my mother" refers to, and map the generations. When someone says "my mother," it is THEIR mother, not yours.

## 4. Coding-Decoding & Analogies

### Coding-Decoding

**Pattern Types**:
1. **Letter shifting**: Each letter is shifted by a fixed number (e.g., A → C, B → D, etc.)
2. **Reverse coding**: The word is written backwards (e.g., CAT → TAC)
3. **Position-based**: Letters are rearranged based on position (e.g., 1st and last swap, 2nd and 2nd-last swap)
4. **Number coding**: Letters are replaced by numbers (A=1, B=2, etc.)

**Example**:
> "In a certain code, 'COMPUTER' is written as 'EQORWVGT'. How is 'LAPTOP' written in that code?"

**Analysis**:
- C → E (+2)
- O → Q (+2)
- M → O (+2)
- P → R (+2)
- U → W (+2)
- T → V (+2)
- E → G (+2)
- R → T (+2)
- Pattern: Each letter is shifted forward by 2.
- L → N, A → C, P → R, T → V, O → Q, P → R
- **Answer: NCRVQR**

### Analogies

**Structure**: A : B :: C : ?  
**Task**: Find the relationship between A and B, then apply it to C.

**Common Relationship Types**:

| Type | Example | Explanation |
|------|---------|-------------|
| **Synonym** | Happy : Joyful :: Sad : Sorrowful | Same meaning |
| **Antonym** | Hot : Cold :: Rich : Poor | Opposite meaning |
| **Part-Whole** | Wheel : Car :: Page : Book | Part is a component of the whole |
| **Cause-Effect** | Fire : Burn :: Rain : Flood | One causes the other |
| **Degree** | Warm : Hot :: Cool : Cold | One is a lesser degree of the other |
| **Function** | Pen : Write :: Knife : Cut | Tool and its function |
| **Category** | Rose : Flower :: Dog : Animal | Member and category |
| **Sequential** | Monday : Tuesday :: January : February | One follows the other |

**Example**:
> "Doctor : Hospital :: Teacher : ?"

**Analysis**:
- Relationship: A doctor works in a hospital.
- Applying: A teacher works in a **school**.
