## The 3 Passage Types & How to Attack Each

### Type 1: Editorial Passages (Most Common)

**Structure**: Author takes a position on a current issue → presents arguments → concludes with a call to action or prognosis.

| Question Type | What to Look For | CLAT Strategy |
|---------------|------------------|---------------|
| **Main Idea** | The central argument the author is making | NOT the first sentence — it's the thesis that everything supports |
| **Author's Tone** | Sarcastic, critical, appreciative, neutral, polemical | Look for adjectives and qualifiers — "tragically," "ironically," "commendably" |
| **Inference** | What MUST be true but is NOT stated | Eliminate anything that adds new information or contradicts the passage |
| **Fact-Extraction** | What is explicitly stated | Direct quote or paraphrase — do NOT infer |

**Example Trap**: The passage criticizes a government policy. The question asks for the author's tone. 
- ❌ "Neutral" → The author is clearly critical, not neutral
- ❌ "Appreciative" → Opposite of the passage's intent
- ✅ "Critical but measured" → The author presents arguments, not just outrage

### Type 2: Philosophical Passages

**Structure**: Abstract concept → different perspectives → author's synthesis or critique.

**Key Strategy**: Philosophical passages are intentionally dense. Your job is to **map the argument structure**, not understand every word.

```
Paragraph 1: Introduction of concept (e.g., "justice as fairness")
Paragraph 2: Perspective A (Rawls — veil of ignorance)
Paragraph 3: Perspective B (Nozick — entitlement theory)
Paragraph 4: Author's critique or synthesis
```

> **CLAT Strategy**: The author's position is usually in the **last paragraph**. Don't get lost in the abstraction — focus on the structure.

### Type 3: Narrative Passages

**Structure**: Story or anecdote → implicit moral or social commentary.

**Key Strategy**: The "story" is a vehicle for a larger point. The question will test whether you can extract the **underlying theme**.

## The 5-Question Arc (CLAT Pattern)

For every 450-word passage, CLAT asks approximately 5 questions in this order:

| Q# | Type | Difficulty | Strategy |
|----|------|------------|----------|
| 1 | **Main Idea** | Medium | Summarize the passage in 10 words |
| 2 | **Fact-Extraction** | Easy | Locate the explicit statement |
| 3 | **Inference** | Hard | What MUST follow? Eliminate "could be" answers |
| 4 | **Vocabulary in Context** | Medium | Read the sentence BEFORE and AFTER the word |
| 5 | **Tone/Author's Perspective** | Medium | Look for emotional adjectives and rhetorical devices |

## The 90-Second Skim Technique

> **CLAT Reality**: You have ~5 minutes per passage (including 5 questions). You cannot read every word carefully.

**Step 1: Read the FIRST and LAST sentence of each paragraph** (20 seconds)
- This gives you the author's trajectory
- The first paragraph introduces the topic; the last concludes the argument

**Step 2: Read the FIRST and LAST paragraph fully** (30 seconds)
- The first paragraph states the topic
- The last paragraph states the author's position

**Step 3: Skim the middle paragraphs for keywords** (30 seconds)
- Look for transition words: "however," "conversely," "more importantly," "therefore"
- Underline names, dates, and statistics — these are fact-extraction targets

**Step 4: Answer questions WITHOUT re-reading** (2–3 minutes)
- 80% of questions can be answered from your skim
- Only re-read for inference questions that require precise fact-matching

> ⚠️ **CLAT Trap**: Do NOT read the passage first, then read the questions. Skim for 90 seconds, then answer questions as you go. The questions will guide you to the relevant sections.

## Vocabulary in Context: The CLAT Method

CLAT never tests dictionary definitions. It tests **contextual usage**.

**Example Passage**: "The government's response was **merely** perfunctory, offering no substantive solutions to the crisis."

**Question**: What does "perfunctory" mean in this context?

| Option | Analysis | Verdict |
|--------|----------|---------|
| A. Enthusiastic | Opposite of "merely" and "no substantive solutions" | ❌ |
| B. Diligent | Also opposite of "merely" | ❌ |
| C. Superficial or routine | Matches "merely" and "no substantive solutions" | ✅ |
| D. Controversial | Not supported by context | ❌ |

**Key Rule**: The sentence before and after the target word contains the answer. Use the **tone and logic** of the surrounding text to deduce meaning.

---

## The 2-Out Rule (Elimination Strategy)

For every question, **eliminate 2 wrong answers FIRST** before analyzing the remaining 2.

**Why this works**: CLAT options are designed with 2 obviously wrong answers and 2 plausible ones. If you can quickly knock out 2, your accuracy on the remaining 2 will jump 30%.

**How to identify the 2 wrong answers**:
1. **Extreme language**: "always," "never," "all," "none" → usually wrong
2. **Contradicts the passage**: If the passage says X, and an option says not-X, eliminate it
3. **Too specific**: The answer adds detail not in the passage
4. **Too broad**: The answer is true but doesn't answer the specific question
