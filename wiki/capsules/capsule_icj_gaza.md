## The Problem: Why This Case Happened

**Main Contextual Influence**: The geopolitical fallout of the October 7, 2023 attacks and the subsequent massive military campaign in Gaza. The absolute collapse of **United Nations Security Council (UNSC) enforcement mechanisms** due to continuous vetoes forced the Global South to weaponize formal international legal tribunals to check military actions.

## Core Legal Underpinnings

### 1. Erga Omnes Partes Obligations

**Erga omnes** is a Latin phrase meaning **"towards all."** In international law, erga omnes obligations are obligations that a state owes to the international community as a whole, not just to another state.

South Africa had **no direct, localized injury** in the conflict. It established its standing (locus standi) on the doctrine that **all state parties to the 1948 Genocide Convention** have a common legal interest in ensuring the prevention and punishment of acts of genocide anywhere in the world.

| Concept | Meaning | Application |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Erga Omnes Partes** | Obligations owed to all parties of a treaty | Every Genocide Convention state has standing to sue |
| **Locus Standi** | Right to bring a case | South Africa sued as a guarantor, not as a victim |
| **No direct injury required** | Unlike domestic tort law | International law permits "community interest" standing |

> **CLAT Strategy**: If a passage asks why South Africa could bring the case despite no direct injury, the answer is **erga omnes partes obligations** under the Genocide Convention. A passage might present another state (e.g., Bangladesh, Ireland) filing a similar case and ask whether locus standi exists. The answer is **YES** — any Genocide Convention party has standing.

### 2. The Threshold of Plausibility

At the **provisional measures stage**, the ICJ did **not** rule on whether genocide had occurred. Instead, it ruled that the rights claimed by South Africa under the convention were **"plausible,"** creating an immediate international legal obligation on Israel to prevent escalations that endanger civilian life.

| Stage | Standard | What the Court Decides |
|-------|----------|----------------------|
| **Provisional Measures** | **Plausibility** | Are the claimed rights plausible? → Issue emergency orders |
| **Merits (Final Judgment)** | **Proof** | Did genocide actually occur? → Full factual and legal determination |

> **CLAT Strategy**: The "plausibility" threshold is the most-tested procedural nuance. The ICJ at provisional measures stage does **NOT** determine guilt — it only asks if the claimed right is plausible. This distinguishes provisional measures from a merits ruling. The passage will almost certainly test this distinction.

## CLAT Passage Strategy

### The Three Things You Must Know

1. **The exact trigger** (Dec 29, 2023 — South Africa files; Jan 26, 2024 — ICJ provisional measures)
2. **The constitutional/legal provision at stake** (1948 Genocide Convention, erga omnes partes obligations)
3. **The competing interest that was overridden and why** (UNSC deadlock due to vetoes → ICJ became the forum; state sovereignty vs. international humanitarian law)

### How CLAT Will Test This

- **Erga Omnes**: A passage might present another state filing a similar case and ask whether locus standi exists. The answer is **YES** — any Genocide Convention party has standing under erga omnes partes.
- **Plausibility vs. Proof**: The ICJ's provisional measures are **NOT** a final judgment. They are emergency orders. The threshold is **"plausibility"** — not proof of genocide.
- **UNSC Deadlock**: The **Security Council veto power** is why this case went to the ICJ instead of the UNSC. This is a key geopolitical point.
- **Provisional Measures**: These are **binding** emergency orders to prevent irreparable harm while the case is heard. Israel was ordered to prevent escalations endangering civilian life.

### Key Geopolitical Context

The UNSC's failure to act (due to vetoes) created a **legal vacuum** that the Global South filled by going to the ICJ. This is a key theme in international law passages: **when political institutions fail, legal institutions step in.**
