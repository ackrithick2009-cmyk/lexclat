## The Anatomy of an Argument (Know Cold)

Every CLAT argument has 3 components:

```
PREMISE (Evidence) → ASSUMPTION (Unstated Link) → CONCLUSION (Claim)
```

**Example Passage**:
> "India's rural literacy rate has increased from 60% to 75% in the last decade. **Therefore**, government investment in rural education has been effective."

| Component | In the Passage | Hidden? |
|-----------|----------------|---------|
| **Premise** | "Rural literacy increased from 60% to 75%" | No |
| **Assumption** | "The increase in literacy is DUE TO government investment, not other factors" | **YES** |
| **Conclusion** | "Government investment in rural education has been effective" | No |

> **CLAT Strategy**: The assumption is the **unstated bridge** between premise and conclusion. If the assumption is false, the conclusion collapses.

---

## Question Type 1: Assumptions (Most Tested)

**Definition**: An assumption is an unstated premise that MUST be true for the conclusion to follow.

**The Negation Test**: Negate each option. If negating the option destroys the conclusion, it is the correct assumption.

**Example**:
> Passage: "Rural literacy increased. Therefore, government investment was effective."

| Option | Negation Test | Is it the Assumption? |
|--------|---------------|---------------------|
| A. Literacy rates measure education quality | "Literacy rates do NOT measure education quality" → conclusion still possible | ❌ |
| B. No other factor caused the literacy increase | "Other factors DID cause the increase" → conclusion collapses | ✅ |
| C. Government spending always improves literacy | "Government spending does NOT always improve literacy" → conclusion still possible | ❌ |
| D. Urban literacy also increased | "Urban literacy did NOT increase" → irrelevant to rural conclusion | ❌ |

> **Key Rule**: The correct assumption is the one that, if false, makes the conclusion impossible.

## Question Type 2: Strengthening & Weakening Arguments

**Strengthen**: Find the option that makes the conclusion MORE likely to be true.
**Weaken**: Find the option that makes the conclusion LESS likely to be true.

**The Causal Link Strategy**: Most arguments in CLAT are **causal** (A causes B). To strengthen/weaken, attack the causal link:

| Attack Vector | Strengthen | Weaken |
|---------------|------------|--------|
| **Alternative Cause** | Rule out other causes | Introduce another cause |
| **Reverse Causation** | Show B does not cause A | Show B actually causes A |
| **Correlation ≠ Causation** | Show A and B are directly linked | Show A and B are merely correlated |
| **Sample Size** | Show the sample is representative | Show the sample is biased or too small |

**Example**:
> Passage: "Cities with more parks have lower crime rates. Therefore, parks reduce crime."

**Strengthen**: "Cities that built parks saw a subsequent decrease in crime, while cities without parks saw no change." (Rules out alternative causes, shows temporal sequence)

**Weaken**: "Wealthier cities build more parks AND have lower crime due to better policing." (Introduces alternative cause: wealth, not parks)

## Question Type 3: Logical Flaws

**Definition**: A flaw is a specific error in the reasoning that makes the conclusion unreliable.

**Top 5 Flaws Tested in CLAT**:

| Flaw | Definition | Example |
|------|------------|---------|
| **Hasty Generalization** | Drawing a broad conclusion from a small sample | "My neighbor got a job through nepotism. Therefore, all government jobs are given through nepotism." |
| **Circular Reasoning** | The conclusion is assumed in the premise | "X is true because X is true." |
| **False Dichotomy** | Presenting only two options when more exist | "Either we ban all social media, or we accept cyberbullying." |
| **Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc** | Assuming causation from correlation | "After the new policy, GDP rose. Therefore, the policy caused GDP growth." |
| **Ad Hominem** | Attacking the person instead of the argument | "You can't trust his economic analysis — he's a politician." |

> **CLAT Strategy**: Identify the flaw BEFORE reading the options. Then match your identified flaw to the option that describes it.

---

## Question Type 4: Paradoxes

**Definition**: A paradox presents two seemingly contradictory facts and asks you to resolve the contradiction.

**Example**:
> "City X has the highest per-capita income in the country. Yet, it also has the highest poverty rate."

**Resolution**: "City X has extreme income inequality — a small wealthy population pulls up the average, while a large poor population drives the poverty rate."

> **Key Strategy**: The correct answer will introduce a **third factor** that explains how both facts can coexist. Do not deny either fact — explain both.

## The 4-Step Passage Attack

For 250-word critical reasoning passages with 5–6 questions:

**Step 1: Identify the Conclusion** (10 seconds)
- Look for conclusion indicators: "therefore," "thus," "consequently," "it follows that"
- The conclusion is the author's main claim

**Step 2: Identify the Premises** (10 seconds)
- Look for premise indicators: "because," "since," "given that," "as shown by"
- These are the evidence supporting the conclusion

**Step 3: Map the Assumption** (10 seconds)
- What MUST be true for the premises to support the conclusion?
- If you can articulate the assumption, you can answer 80% of the questions

**Step 4: Answer the Questions** (3–4 minutes)
- Assumption questions → Use the negation test
- Strengthen/Weaken → Attack the causal link
- Flaw → Match the flaw type
- Inference → What MUST be true, not what COULD be true
