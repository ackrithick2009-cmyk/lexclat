## 1. Landmark Constitutional Cases

| Case | Year | Significance |
|------|------|--------------|
| **Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala** | 1973 | Basic Structure Doctrine — Parliament cannot amend fundamental features of the Constitution |
| **Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India** | 1978 | Expanded Art. 21: "procedure established by law" = fair, just, reasonable |
| **Minerva Mills v. Union of India** | 1980 | Reaffirmed Basic Structure; balance between Fundamental Rights & DPSPs |
| **S.R. Bommai v. Union of India** | 1994 | Federalism is part of the Basic Structure; limited use of Art. 356 |
| **Vishaka v. State of Rajasthan** | 1997 | Sexual harassment at workplace; guidelines became law via POSH Act 2013 |
| **Navtej Singh Johar v. Union of India** | 2018 | Decriminalised consensual same-sex relations; Section 377 IPC partially struck down |
| **Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India** | 2017 | Right to Privacy is a Fundamental Right under Art. 21 |
| **Indra Sawhney v. Union of India** | 1992 | 50% cap on reservations; creamy layer exclusion for OBCs |

### The "LegalEdge" Principle-Application Framework
> *Adopted from TopRankers' premier methodology.*
> CLAT gives a **passage stating a legal principle**, then asks you to **apply it to a factual scenario**. You are NOT tested on memorisation alone.

**The 3-Step Strategy:**
1. **Isolate the Principle**: Read the passage and identify the exact conditions required for the rule to apply. Ignore the fluff.
2. **Strict Adherence**: Assume the principle given in the passage is the *absolute truth*, even if it contradicts real-world law. **NEVER import outside knowledge**.
3. **Fact-Matching**: Check off each condition from the principle against the facts. If even one condition is missing, the rule does not apply.

## 2. Criminal Law

### Key Concepts from IPC / BNS 2023
> ⚠️ The **Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) 2023** replaced IPC from July 2024. CLAT 2025 onwards tests BNS terminology.

| Concept | Definition | BNS Section |
|---------|-----------|-------------|
| **Actus Reus** | Guilty act (physical element of crime) | General Principles |
| **Mens Rea** | Guilty mind (intent) | S. 13–18 BNS |
| **Murder vs. Culpable Homicide** | Murder = intention + knowledge that death will result; CH = lesser intent | S. 99–105 BNS |
| **Attempt** | Act done with intent to commit offence, gone beyond mere preparation | S. 61 BNS |
| **Common Intention** | Shared intent among multiple persons; all equally liable | S. 3(5) BNS |
| **Theft vs. Robbery vs. Dacoity** | Degree of force and number of persons involved | S. 303–310 BNS |
| **Defamation** | False statement injuring reputation; civil AND criminal | S. 356 BNS |

### Private Defence (S. 34–44 BNS)
- Right exists for **body and property**
- Must be **proportional** — cannot cause more harm than necessary
- Right extends to causing death **only** in cases of murder, grievous hurt, rape, kidnapping, acid attack

## 3. Law of Torts

### Foundational Principles

| Principle | Rule |
|-----------|------|
| **Damnum Sine Injuria** | Damage without legal injury → no tort (e.g., lawful competition) |
| **Injuria Sine Damno** | Legal injury without actual damage → actionable (e.g., trespass) |
| **Volenti Non Fit Injuria** | Plaintiff consented to the risk → no liability |
| **Contributory Negligence** | Plaintiff's own negligence contributed to injury → damages reduced |
| **Strict Liability** | *Rylands v. Fletcher*: keeping dangerous things; liable even without fault |
| **Absolute Liability** | *M.C. Mehta v. UOI*: no exceptions; applies to hazardous enterprises in India |

### Negligence — Donoghue v. Stevenson (1932)
Three elements that must ALL be present:
1. **Duty of Care** — existed between defendant and plaintiff
2. **Breach** — defendant fell below the standard of a "reasonable person"
3. **Damage** — plaintiff actually suffered damage caused by the breach

## 4. Contract Law

> Based on the **Indian Contract Act, 1872**

### Essentials of a Valid Contract (S. 10)
1. Offer + Acceptance (S. 2a–2b)
2. Lawful Consideration (S. 2d)
3. Capacity to Contract (S. 11)
4. Free Consent — no coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation, mistake (S. 13–22)
5. Lawful Object (S. 23)
6. Not expressly void (S. 26–30)

### CLAT-Critical Distinctions

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Void Agreement** | Never enforceable from the start (e.g., agreement with minor) |
| **Voidable Contract** | Valid until the aggrieved party rescinds (e.g., contract under coercion) |
| **Void Contract** | Was valid but became unenforceable later |
| **Quasi-Contract** | Obligation imposed by law even without agreement (S. 68–72) |
| **Specific Performance** | Court orders party to perform; available when damages are inadequate |

## 5. 2026 Legislative Update 🆕

### India AI Governance Framework (2025)
- The **Digital India Act (DIA)** draft proposes a **tiered risk-based framework** for AI systems
- **High-risk AI** (healthcare, judiciary, law enforcement) requires mandatory human oversight
- **Principle of Algorithmic Accountability**: Organizations must explain automated decisions affecting individuals' rights
- *CLAT Angle*: Questions may present scenarios about algorithmic bias and test principles of natural justice (Art. 14)

### Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA) 2023 — Evidence Updates
- Replaced the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 from July 1, 2024
- **S. 61**: Electronic Records are admissible; no longer require a certificate in the same manner
- **S. 57**: Expert opinion on digital forensics now explicitly covered

### Telecommunications Act 2023
- Replaced the Indian Telegraph Act 1885
- Government can **intercept communications** in the interest of national security under S. 20
- ISPs must retain user data for a specified period — privacy implications under Art. 21

### Key Supreme Court Judgments (2025)
- **Electoral Bonds Case** (2024): Struck down as violative of Art. 19(1)(a) — voters' right to know
- **Ayodhya Title Dispute follow-up orders** — mosque construction and trust updates
- **LGBTQ+ Marriage Recognition**: SC referred to Parliament — status remains *sub judice*
