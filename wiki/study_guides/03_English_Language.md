# 📖 CLAT English Language — Complete Study Guide

> **Section Weight**: 22–26 questions | ~22 marks | **25 minutes**  
> **Core Skill**: Comprehend 450-word passages from journalism/literature; infer meaning, tone, and structure.

---

## How CLAT Tests English

CLAT English is **100% passage-based** — no standalone grammar MCQs. Each passage (~450 words) has 4–5 questions.

| Question Type | Frequency | Difficulty |
|--------------|-----------|-----------|
| Main Idea / Central Theme | High | Medium |
| Inference | Very High | Hard |
| Contextual Vocabulary | High | Medium |
| Author's Tone / Perspective | High | Medium |
| Strengthen / Weaken (passage argument) | Medium | Hard |
| Grammar (embedded in passage options) | Medium | Medium |

### The Active Reading Protocol

1. **Read questions FIRST** (30 seconds) — primes your attention
2. **Read passage actively** (2 minutes) — mark main idea, tone, transitions
3. **Answer questions** (2–3 minutes) — eliminate wrong options aggressively
4. **Speed target**: 450 words in **under 2 minutes**

## PART A: READING COMPREHENSION

### A1. Main Idea & Central Theme

**Main Idea** = What is the passage **primarily about**?  
**Central Theme** = The **underlying message** or argument.

| Trap | How to Avoid |
|------|-------------|
| Too narrow (one detail) | Ask: "Does this cover the WHOLE passage?" |
| Too broad (beyond scope) | Ask: "Is this actually discussed?" |
| Confuses example with thesis | First + last paragraphs usually hold the key |

**Technique**: After reading, complete: *"This passage argues that __________."*

### A2. Inference Questions

**Inference** = What **logically follows** from the passage but is NOT stated explicitly.

| Valid Inference | Invalid "Inference" |
|----------------|---------------------|
| Supported by multiple sentences | Requires outside knowledge |
| Conservative — "may," "likely," "suggests" | Absolute claims not in passage |
| Stays within passage scope | Goes beyond evidence |

**Example Passage Snippet**:
> "The government has increased the minimum wage by 15%. Prices at local grocery stores have risen 8% this month."

- ✅ **Valid**: Workers may not see a real increase in purchasing power.
- ❌ **Invalid**: The government caused inflation (causation not stated).

### A3. Author's Tone, Style & Perspective

| Tone | Signal Words | Example Context |
|------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Critical** | "However," "flawed," "inadequate," "fail" | Policy critique |
| **Optimistic** | "Promising," "potential," "progress," "hope" | Reform advocacy |
| **Sceptical** | "Claims," "allegedly," "purported," "questionable" | Investigative piece |
| **Neutral/Objective** | Factual reporting; balanced both sides | News analysis |
| **Cautionary** | "Risk," "warning," "danger," "must not" | Regulatory debate |
| **Appreciative** | "Commendable," "notable," "landmark" | Achievement reporting |
| **Sarcastic/Ironic** | Saying opposite of literal meaning | Satirical editorial |

**Style markers**:
- **Formal**: Complex sentences, passive voice, academic vocabulary
- **Conversational**: Contractions, direct address, shorter sentences
- **Literary**: Metaphors, imagery, narrative structure

### A4. Fact-Extraction & Structural Summaries

**Fact-extraction**: Locate explicit information — dates, numbers, names, definitions stated in text.

**Structural summary**: How is the passage organised?

| Structure | Pattern |
|-----------|---------|
| **Problem-Solution** | Issue → analysis → proposed fix |
| **Compare-Contrast** | Two viewpoints or approaches |
| **Cause-Effect** | Event → consequences |
| **Chronological** | Timeline of developments |
| **Thesis-Evidence** | Claim → supporting examples |

## PART B: VOCABULARY IN CONTEXT

### B1. Contextual Meaning Strategy

CLAT tests what a word means **in that specific paragraph**, not its dictionary definition.

**The Substitution Technique**:
1. Read the sentence containing the word
2. Replace the word with each option
3. The correct option preserves the sentence's meaning

**Example**:
> "The court was **cognizant** of the constitutional implications."

- cognizant = **aware and informed** (NOT "cognitive" or "recognised formally")

## PART C: APPLIED GRAMMAR

> Grammar is tested **within passage answer options**, not as standalone questions.

### C1. Subject-Verb Agreement

| Rule | Example |
|------|---------|
| Singular subject → singular verb | "The committee **has** decided" |
| Collective nouns (team, jury) → usually singular | "The jury **is** deliberating" |
| "Each/Every/Neither/Either" → singular | "Each student **has** a book" |
| Compound subjects with "and" → plural | "Ravi and Priya **are** coming" |
| "A number of" → plural; "The number of" → singular | "A number of issues **were** raised" |
| Intervening phrases don't change subject | "The box of apples **is** heavy" |

### C2. Tenses

| Error Pattern | Correction |
|--------------|-----------|
| "Since 2020, he works here" | "Since 2020, he **has worked** here" (since + present perfect) |
| "When I arrived, he left" | "When I arrived, he **had left**" (earlier past = past perfect) |
| "If I was rich, I would travel" | "If I **were** rich..." (subjunctive for hypothetical) |

### C3. Modifiers

**Dangling Modifier** — Modifier doesn't logically attach to any noun:
- ❌ "Walking to school, the rain started."
- ✅ "Walking to school, **I noticed** the rain started."

**Misplaced Modifier** — Modifier in wrong position changes meaning:
- ❌ "She served biscuits to children on paper plates." (children on plates?)
- ✅ "She served biscuits **on paper plates** to children."

### C4. Parallelism

Items in a list or comparison must have **same grammatical form**:
- ❌ "She likes reading, to swim, and jogging."
- ✅ "She likes **reading, swimming, and jogging**."

### C5. Prepositions (Common Errors)

| Correct | Incorrect |
|---------|-----------|
| **Capable of** | Capable to |
| **Comply with** | Comply to |
| **Different from** | Different than (in formal English) |
| **In accordance with** | In accordance to |
| **Prefer X to Y** | Prefer X over Y (formal) |
| **Averse to** | Averse from |

## PART D: PRACTICE PASSAGE WITH QUESTIONS

### Passage: Algorithmic Accountability in Public Governance

> The emergence of algorithmic decision-making across criminal justice, welfare distribution, and financial credit scoring has compelled legal scholars to revisit foundational principles of due process. When a citizen is denied bail because a risk-assessment algorithm flagged a statistical correlation between their socioeconomic background and recidivism, the question is not merely technical — it is profoundly constitutional. The opacity of these systems — often described as 'black boxes' — renders the right to a reasoned administrative decision, long enshrined in natural justice, effectively hollow. Reformers argue that a principle of 'algorithmic accountability' must be codified: any AI system making consequential public decisions must be auditable, explainable, and subject to human override. Critics counter that overregulation risks stifling innovation and that courts have historically adapted to new evidence standards. The balance struck will define the contours of administrative law for a generation.

**Q1. What is the central theme of the passage?**
- (A) AI will replace human judges in the near future
- (B) Algorithmic decision-making raises constitutional concerns about due process and transparency
- (C) Risk-assessment algorithms are always inaccurate
- (D) Innovation should never be regulated

**Answer: (B)**

**Q2. The word "opacity" in context most nearly means:**
- (A) Complexity of code
- (B) Lack of transparency and openness to scrutiny
- (C) High computational cost
- (D) Speed of processing

**Answer: (B)**

**Q3. Which can be inferred from the passage?**
- (A) All bail denials based on algorithms are unconstitutional
- (B) Reformers believe explainability is essential for systems affecting individual rights
- (C) Courts have rejected all AI evidence
- (D) Recidivism cannot be statistically predicted

**Answer: (B)**

**Q4. The author's tone is best described as:**
- (A) Sarcastic and dismissive
- (B) Analytical and balanced, presenting both reform and counter-arguments
- (C) Purely optimistic about AI
- (D) Angry and polemical

**Answer: (B)**

**Q5. Which option would WEAKEN the reformers' argument?**
- (A) Studies show algorithmic systems reduce bias compared to human decision-makers
- (B) Several countries have adopted algorithmic accountability laws
- (C) Black-box systems have been used for decades in aviation safely
- (D) Citizens have a right to know the basis of decisions affecting them

**Answer: (A)** — If algorithms reduce bias, the need for strict accountability may be less compelling.

## PART E: PARA-JUMBLES & SENTENCE COMPLETION

### Para-Jumble Strategy

1. **Find the opening sentence** — Introduces topic; no unexplained pronouns ("it," "this," "they")
2. **Find mandatory pairs** — Sentence A introduces concept; B explains it
3. **Find the conclusion** — "Thus," "Therefore," "Hence," "Ultimately"

**Practice**: Arrange A–E:
- **(A)** The right to a fair trial is not merely a procedural guarantee but a substantive constitutional right.
- **(B)** This principle has been eroded in recent years by delays that render justice practically inaccessible.
- **(C)** Pendency figures show that over 50 million cases await adjudication across Indian courts.
- **(D)** Justice delayed, as Gladstone observed, is justice denied.
- **(E)** Reform of the judicial system must therefore be treated as a matter of constitutional urgency.

**Answer: A → D → B → C → E**

### Sentence Completion Tips

- Check **grammar continuity** (tense, subject-verb)
- Check **logical flow** (cause-effect, contrast markers)
- **Contrast markers**: However, Nevertheless, Despite → expect opposite idea before/after
- **Continuation markers**: Moreover, Furthermore, Additionally → same direction

## PART F: EXAM STRATEGY

### Skimming Technique (90-Second Read)

1. Read **first paragraph** fully (sets thesis)
2. Read **first sentence of each body paragraph** (topic sentences)
3. Read **last paragraph** fully (conclusion)
4. Return to details only when a question demands it

### Elimination Strategy

1. Eliminate options **contradicting the passage**
2. Eliminate options **too extreme** ("always," "never," "all")
3. Eliminate options **outside scope**
4. Between two remaining: pick the **more conservative** inference
