# ⚖️ CLAT Legal Reasoning — Complete Study Guide

> **Section Weight**: 28–32 questions | ~28 marks | **35 minutes**  
> **Core Skill**: Extract a legal principle from a passage and apply it **strictly** to facts. You do NOT need a law degree.

---

## How CLAT Tests Legal Reasoning

| What CLAT Does | What CLAT Does NOT Do |
|----------------|----------------------|
| Gives you a **principle in the passage** | Test deep statutory section numbers |
| Asks you to **match facts to conditions** | Expect you to import outside legal knowledge |
| Tests **logical application** | Reward memorisation of case names alone |

### The 3-Step Principle-Application Method

1. **Isolate the Principle** — Underline every condition that must be satisfied for the rule to apply.
2. **Strict Adherence** — Treat the passage principle as absolute truth, even if real law differs.
3. **Fact-Matching** — Check each condition against the facts. One missing condition = rule does not apply.

## PART A: LAW OF TORTS

### A1. Foundational Concepts

| Term | Meaning | CLAT Example |
|------|---------|--------------|
| **Tort** | Civil wrong causing harm; remedy = damages | Neighbour sues for noise pollution |
| **Damnum Sine Injuria** | Damage without legal injury → **no tort** | Lawful competition hurts your business |
| **Injuria Sine Damno** | Legal injury without actual loss → **actionable** | Trespass on land even if no damage |
| **Ubi Jus Ibi Remedium** | Where there is a right, there is a remedy | Latin maxim — frequently tested |

### A2. Negligence

**Elements (ALL must be present):**
1. **Duty of Care** — Defendant owed a duty to the plaintiff
2. **Breach** — Fell below the standard of a "reasonable person"
3. **Causation** — Breach caused the harm (causa causans)
4. **Damage** — Actual loss or injury suffered

**Res Ipsa Loquitur** ("The thing speaks for itself"):
- Applied when the accident is of a kind that **ordinarily does not happen without negligence**
- **Burden shifts** to defendant to prove they were NOT negligent
- Example: Surgical instrument left inside patient after operation

**Contributory Negligence**: Plaintiff's own carelessness contributed → damages **reduced proportionally**.

**Practice — Negligence**

> **Principle**: A person who fails to take reasonable care to avoid acts or omissions which they can reasonably foresee would likely injure their neighbour is liable for negligence.
>
> **Facts**: Ravi, a shopkeeper, leaves a wet floor without a warning sign. Priya slips and fractures her arm. Ravi argues he was busy and forgot.
>
> **Q**: Is Ravi liable?
> **A**: **Yes**. Duty of care (customers as neighbours), breach (no warning sign), causation (slip due to wet floor), damage (fracture). Foreseeability is satisfied.

### A3. Defamation

| Type | Medium | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| **Libel** | Permanent/written form | Newspaper article, social media post |
| **Slander** | Transient/spoken form | False spoken accusation |

**Essentials:**
1. Statement must be **published** (communicated to at least one third party)
2. Statement must refer to the **plaintiff**
3. Statement must be **defamatory** (lowers reputation in eyes of reasonable person)
4. Statement must be **false** (truth is a complete defence)

**Defences**: Truth (justification), Fair comment, Privilege (absolute/qualified), Consent.

---

### A4. Strict Liability vs Absolute Liability

| | **Strict Liability** (Rylands v Fletcher) | **Absolute Liability** (M.C. Mehta v UOI) |
|--|------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------|
| **Rule** | Person who keeps dangerous thing on land liable if it escapes | Hazardous/industry liable for ANY harm — **no exceptions** |
| **Exceptions** | Act of God, plaintiff's fault, third party, consent | **None** in India for hazardous enterprises |
| **Fault required?** | No — liability without proof of negligence | No |
| **Indian context** | General rule | Oleum gas leak case — Bhopal principle extended |

---

### A5. Vicarious Liability

**Rule**: One person (master/employer) held liable for torts committed by another (servant/agent) **in course of employment**.

| Relationship | Liability? |
|-------------|-----------|
| Employer → Employee (during duty) | **Yes** |
| Employer → Employee (personal frolic) | **No** |
| Independent contractor | Generally **No** (exceptions: inherently dangerous work) |
| Partnership | Partners jointly liable for firm's torts |

**CLAT Trap**: "During course of employment" is key — a delivery driver hitting someone while delivering packages = vicarious liability. Same driver hitting someone on a personal weekend trip = no.

---

### A6. Nuisance

| Type | Definition | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|
| **Private Nuisance** | Unlawful interference with use/enjoyment of land | Excessive noise from factory next door |
| **Public Nuisance** | Act affecting rights of public at large | Blocking a public road |

**Remedies**: Injunction, damages, abatement (self-help in limited cases).

**Balancing test**: Courts weigh utility of defendant's act vs harm to plaintiff.

---

### A7. Malicious Prosecution

**Essentials (ALL required):**
1. Prosecution was **instituted** by defendant
2. Prosecution ended in **favour** of the plaintiff (acquittal/dismissal)
3. No **reasonable or probable cause** for prosecution
4. Prosecution was with **malice** (improper motive, not merely bad judgment)
5. Plaintiff suffered **damage** (reputation, liberty, financial)

---

### A8. General Defences in Tort

| Defence | Rule | Example |
|---------|------|---------|
| **Volenti Non Fit Injuria** | No injury to one who consents | Boxing match injury with informed consent |
| **Act of God** | Natural disaster beyond human control | Flood destroys crops — no liability if unforeseeable |
| **Inevitable Accident** | Could not be prevented despite reasonable care | Sudden heart attack while driving |
| **Plaintiff the Wrongdoer** | Cannot recover if plaintiff was also at fault | Thief injured while trespassing |
| **Statutory Authority** | Act authorised by law | Government demolishing unsafe building under statute |
| **Private Defence** | Reasonable force to protect person/property | See Criminal Law section |

**Volenti — Critical Distinction**: Consent must be **free and informed**. A worker forced to work in unsafe conditions has NOT consented.

## PART B: LAW OF CONTRACTS (Indian Contract Act, 1872)

### B1. Offer and Acceptance

| Concept | Rule |
|---------|------|
| **Offer** (S. 2a) | Signifies willingness to enter contract on stated terms |
| **Acceptance** (S. 2b) | Unqualified assent to offer |
| **Communication** | Offer/acceptance/revocation must be **communicated** |
| **Revocation** | Offer revoked before acceptance; acceptance before revocation of acceptance |
| **Counter-offer** | Kills original offer — becomes new offer |
| **Cross offers** | Two identical offers crossing in post → **no contract** |

**Invitation to Treat** ≠ Offer: Menu, catalogue, auction display = invitation; customer makes the offer.

### B2. Consideration (S. 2d)

- Something in **return** — can be act, forbearance, or promise
- Must be **lawful**
- **Past consideration** is valid in India (unlike English law in some contexts)
- **Adequacy** of consideration need not be equal (₹1 for a house can be valid if voluntary)

### B3. Capacity to Contract (S. 11)

| Person | Capacity |
|--------|----------|
| **Major** (18+) | Full capacity |
| **Minor** | Agreement **void** — cannot enforce OR be enforced |
| **Person of unsound mind** | Void while incapacitated |
| **Disqualified persons** | e.g., alien enemy during war |

**Mohiri Bibee Rule**: Minor's agreement is **void ab initio** — not voidable.

### B4. Free Consent (S. 13–22)

| Vitiating Factor | Effect |
|-----------------|--------|
| **Coercion** (S. 15) | Voidable at option of aggrieved party |
| **Undue Influence** (S. 16) | Voidable — domination of will |
| **Fraud** (S. 17) | Voidable — active deception |
| **Misrepresentation** (S. 18) | Voidable — innocent false statement |
| **Mistake of Fact** (S. 20) | **Void** if both parties mistake essential fact |
| **Mistake of Law** | Generally **no relief** (except foreign law) |

### B5. Void, Voidable & Illegal

| Category | Meaning | Example |
|----------|---------|---------|
| **Void Agreement** | Never enforceable | Agreement with minor |
| **Voidable Contract** | Valid until rescinded | Contract under coercion |
| **Void Contract** | Was valid, became unenforceable | Subsequent impossibility |
| **Illegal Agreement** | Forbidden by law | Contract to commit crime |

### B6. Breach & Remedies

| Remedy | When Available |
|--------|---------------|
| **Damages** | Compensation for loss — most common |
| **Specific Performance** | Court orders actual performance — when damages inadequate (unique property) |
| **Injunction** | Restraining order — prevent breach |
| **Quantum Meruit** | Reasonable payment for work done when contract fails |
| **Anticipatory Breach** | One party repudiates before due date — other party can sue immediately |

**Types of Damages**: General (natural loss), Special (known unusual loss), Nominal (symbolic), Liquidated (pre-agreed sum).

## PART C: CRIMINAL LAW (BNS 2023)

> ⚠️ **Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS)** replaced IPC from **July 1, 2024**. CLAT tests BNS terminology.

### C1. Elements of Crime

| Element | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| **Actus Reus** | Guilty act — physical conduct |
| **Mens Rea** | Guilty mind — intention, knowledge, recklessness |
| **Concurrence** | Act and mind must coincide |
| **Causation** | Act must cause the prohibited result |

**General Rule**: Both actus reus AND mens rea required (strict liability offences are exceptions).

### C2. General Exceptions (BNS S. 34–44)

| Exception | Key Rule |
|-----------|----------|
| **Private Defence** | Proportional force to protect body/property; death-causing force only in grave situations (murder, rape, kidnapping, acid attack) |
| **Insanity** (McNaughten Rule) | Did not know nature/quality of act OR that act was wrong |
| **Minor** | Below 7 = no offence; 7–12 = if mature understanding; 12–18 = JJ Act applies |
| **Intoxication** | Involuntary intoxication may excuse; voluntary generally does not |
| **Accident** | Act without criminal intent, lawful manner, due care |
| **Consent** | Not offence if consent given (exceptions: grievous hurt, death) |

### C3. Murder vs Culpable Homicide (BNS S. 99–105)

| | **Culpable Homicide** | **Murder** |
|--|----------------------|-----------|
| **Intent** | Knowledge death likely OR intention to cause bodily injury likely to cause death | Intention to cause death OR knowledge that act is so imminently dangerous it must cause death |
| **Gravity** | Lesser degree | "Aggravated" culpable homicide |
| **Punishment** | Up to life imprisonment | Death or life imprisonment |

**CLAT Strategy**: Check if facts show **specific intention to kill** → Murder. If only knowledge that death *may* result → Culpable Homicide.

### C4. Theft, Extortion, Robbery, Dacoity

| Offence | Essentials |
|---------|-----------|
| **Theft** (S. 303) | Dishonest moving of movable property without consent |
| **Extortion** (S. 308) | Intentionally putting person in fear of injury to deliver property |
| **Robbery** (S. 309) | Theft + force/threat OR extortion on spot |
| **Dacoity** (S. 310) | Robbery by 5+ persons |

**Theft vs Extortion**: Theft = taking without consent. Extortion = consent obtained through **fear**.

### C5. BNS Key Transformations (IPC → BNS)

| Topic | IPC | BNS |
|-------|-----|-----|
| Sedition | S. 124A | **Repealed** → S. 152 (acts endangering sovereignty) |
| Mob Lynching | Not distinct | **S. 103(2)** — life or death |
| Terrorism | Not in general law | **S. 113** — defined |
| Snatching | Not defined | **S. 304** — new offence |
| Community Service | Not formal | Introduced for petty offences |
| Defamation | S. 499 | **S. 356** |

## PART D: CONSTITUTIONAL LAW

### D1. Preamble

- **Not enforceable** in court by itself, but guides interpretation
- Keywords: **Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic, Republic**
- **Justice** — social, economic, political
- **Liberty** — thought, expression, belief, faith, worship
- **Equality** — status and opportunity
- **Fraternity** — dignity and unity

### D2. Fundamental Rights (Articles 12–35)

| Article | Right | Key Point |
|---------|-------|-----------|
| **14** | Equality before law | Reasonable classification permitted; not arbitrary |
| **15** | Non-discrimination | Prohibits discrimination on religion, race, caste, sex, place of birth |
| **16** | Equality in public employment | Reservation permitted |
| **19(1)(a)** | Freedom of speech | Subject to Art. 19(2) restrictions |
| **19(1)(g)** | Practice any profession | Subject to reasonable restrictions |
| **20** | Protection in conviction | No ex-post-facto law, double jeopardy, self-incrimination |
| **21** | Life and personal liberty | Expanded: privacy, dignity, clean environment, speedy trial |
| **21A** | Right to Education | 6–14 years (86th Amendment) |
| **23** | Human trafficking prohibited | Forced labour banned |
| **24** | Child labour | Below 14 in hazardous industries |
| **25–28** | Religious freedom | Subject to public order, morality, health |
| **29–30** | Cultural/educational rights | Minority institutions protected |
| **32** | Constitutional remedies | **Heart and soul** — Dr. Ambedkar; writ jurisdiction of SC |

**Golden Triangle**: Articles **14, 19, 21** read together for FR analysis.

### D3. Directive Principles (Articles 36–51)

| Feature | FR | DPSP |
|---------|-----|------|
| Enforceability | **Justiciable** | **Non-justiciable** (guidelines for State) |
| Nature | Individual rights | Socio-economic goals |
| Conflict | FR prevails in direct conflict (generally) | State policy direction |

**Key DPSPs**: Equal pay for equal work (Art. 39), Free legal aid (Art. 39A), Uniform Civil Code (Art. 44), Panchayati Raj (Art. 40).

### D4. Fundamental Duties (Article 51A)

- Added by **42nd Amendment (1976)**; expanded by **86th Amendment**
- **11 duties** — not enforceable by courts, but guide interpretation
- Includes: respect Constitution, promote harmony, protect environment, develop scientific temper

### D5. President & Governor

| Power | President (Art. 52–78) | Governor (Art. 153–167) |
|-------|----------------------|------------------------|
| **Appointment** | Elected by Electoral College | Appointed by President |
| **Term** | 5 years | At pleasure of President |
| **Ordinance** | Art. 123 — when Parliament not in session | Art. 213 — when State Legislature not in session |
| **Pardon** | Art. 72 — all cases including death, court-martial | Art. 161 — state offences, not death/court-martial |
| **Emergency** | Proclaims National Emergency (Art. 352) | Reports for President's Rule (Art. 356) |

### D6. Structure of Judiciary

| Court | Jurisdiction |
|-------|-------------|
| **Supreme Court** | Highest; original (Centre-State), appellate, advisory, writ (Art. 32) |
| **High Court** | State level; writ (Art. 226 — wider than SC), superintendence over subordinate courts |
| **District Courts** | Original civil/criminal jurisdiction |

**Collegium System**: SC judges appointed by President on recommendation of CJI + senior judges.

### D7. Writs (Articles 32 & 226)

| Writ | Meaning | Use |
|------|---------|-----|
| **Habeas Corpus** | "Produce the body" | Illegal detention |
| **Mandamus** | "We command" | Public official fails duty |
| **Prohibition** | Stop lower court | Excess/ lack of jurisdiction |
| **Certiorari** | Quash order | Judicial/quasi-judicial excess |
| **Quo Warranto** | "By what authority?" | Illegal public office holder |

### D8. Emergency Provisions

| Type | Article | Ground | Effect |
|------|---------|--------|--------|
| **National Emergency** | 352 | War, external aggression, armed rebellion | Centre gains wide powers; FR (except 20, 21) can be suspended |
| **President's Rule** | 356 | State government cannot function per Constitution | State legislature suspended/dissolved |
| **Financial Emergency** | 360 | Financial stability threatened | President directs state financial measures |

**44th Amendment (1978)**: Art. 20 & 21 cannot be suspended even during emergency.

## PART E: LEGAL MAXIMS (High-Yield)

| Maxim | Meaning | Application |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| **Res Ipsa Loquitur** | Thing speaks for itself | Negligence presumed from nature of accident |
| **Quid Pro Quo** | Something for something | Consideration; bribery ("this for that") |
| **Audi Alteram Partem** | Hear the other side | Natural justice — fair hearing before decision |
| **Nemo Judex in Causa Sua** | No one judge in own cause | Bias rule — decision-maker must be impartial |
| **Ubi Jus Ibi Remedium** | Where right, there is remedy | Every wrong has a remedy |
| **Volenti Non Fit Injuria** | To willing person, no injury | Consent defence |
| **Ignorantia Juris Non Excusat** | Ignorance of law no excuse | Cannot claim unawareness of law |
| **Res Judicata** | Matter already judged | Cannot re-litigate same issue |
| **Actus Non Facit Reum Nisi Mens Sit Rea** | Act alone doesn't make guilty without guilty mind | Mens rea requirement |
| **Pari Passu** | On equal footing | Equal treatment of creditors |

## PART G: PRACTICE SET (10 Questions)

**Q1.** Principle: "A person is vicariously liable only if the tort was committed in the course of employment."
Facts: A taxi company's driver, while on a lunch break (unauthorised), hits a pedestrian.
**Answer**: **Not liable** — personal frolic, not in course of employment.

**Q2.** Principle: "An agreement with a minor is void."
Facts: 17-year-old sells his bicycle for ₹5,000 and later wants it back.
**Answer**: **Void** — minor can recover bicycle; money recovery complicated.

**Q3.** Principle: "Murder requires intention to cause death or knowledge that the act is so imminently dangerous it must cause death."
Facts: A pushes B off a cliff knowing B will die but claiming he only wanted to scare B.
**Answer**: **Murder** — knowledge that act must cause death.

**Q4.** Principle: "Res Ipsa Loquitur applies when the accident is of a kind that does not ordinarily occur without negligence."
Facts: A patient finds scissors inside body after surgery.
**Answer**: **Res Ipsa applies** — burden shifts to hospital.

**Q5.** Principle: "Volenti non fit injuria applies only when consent is free and informed."
Facts: Factory worker signs waiver under threat of termination, then injured.
**Answer**: **Defence fails** — consent not free.

**Q6.** Principle: "Article 19(2) permits reasonable restrictions on speech including public order."
Facts: Speech creates imminent violence; government bans it.
**Answer**: **Likely valid** — public order restriction, proportionate.

**Q7.** Principle: "Extortion requires putting a person in fear of injury to deliver property."
Facts: A threatens to expose B's secret unless B pays ₹10,000. B pays.
**Answer**: **Extortion** — fear of reputational harm + delivery of property.

**Q8.** Principle: "Strict liability under Rylands v Fletcher has exceptions including act of God."
Facts: Chemical stored lawfully; unprecedented earthquake causes leak.
**Answer**: **Act of God exception** — may escape strict liability.

**Q9.** Principle: "Malicious prosecution requires absence of reasonable and probable cause."
Facts: A reports genuine suspicion to police; accused acquitted.
**Answer**: **No malicious prosecution** — reasonable cause existed.

**Q10.** Principle: "Specific performance is granted when damages are inadequate."
Facts: Unique ancestral property sold; seller refuses to transfer.
**Answer**: **Specific performance** — unique property, damages inadequate.

## PART H: HIGHEST-YIELD LEGAL DEVELOPMENTS (2024–2026) — CLAT PASSAGE MASTERCLASS

> ⚠️ **To ace the passage-based format of CLAT, you must understand exactly when these shifts occurred and the legal or geopolitical catalysts that forced them.**
> These topics are **guaranteed** to appear as principle-application passages. They test your ability to extract a legal doctrine from a real-world context and apply it to a hypothetical fact pattern.

---

### H1. The New Criminal Trilogy (BNS, BNSS, BSA) — Decolonial Jurisprudence

**Key Timelines:**
- **December 25, 2023**: Received Presidential Assent.
- **July 1, 2024**: Officially came into force across India (except for specific hit-and-run provisions under BNS Section 106(2) due to transport strikes).

**Main Contextual Influence:** The overarching driver was a structural shift from colonial-era retributive policing to modern, technology-driven restorative justice. The IPC (1860), CrPC (1973), and Evidence Act (1872) were fundamentally unequipped to handle digital forensics, cloud-stored data, or decentralized organized crime networks.

**Core Legal Underpinnings:**

1. **Decolonial Jurisprudence**: Replacing Sedition (Old IPC Sec 124A) with Acts Endangering Sovereignty (BNS Sec 152) to remove the colonial construct of criminalizing mere disaffection toward the government, shifting the focus strictly to threats against the state's integrity.

2. **Evidentiary Modernization**: The BSA updates Section 57 to recognize electronic records as primary evidence. This directly integrates the judicial framework established in *Anvar P.V. v. P.K. Basheer* (2014) regarding certificate requirements for electronic data.

**CLAT Passage Strategy:**
- When a passage mentions "electronic evidence" or "digital forensics," look for references to *Anvar P.V.* and the BSA's evidentiary modernization.
- When a passage discusses "sedition" or "disaffection," the answer is likely about BNS Section 152 and the shift from colonial "sedition" to "endangering sovereignty."
- The BNS reduced the number of offenses from 511 to 358. This decriminalization trend is a key theme.

---

### H2. SC/ST Sub-Classification (State of Punjab v. Davinder Singh) — Substantive Equality

**Key Timeline:**
- **August 1, 2024**: The 7-judge Constitution Bench of the Supreme Court delivered its landmark 6:1 majority ruling.

**Main Contextual Influence:** Decades of administrative data revealed that within the Scheduled Castes umbrella, a few relatively advanced sub-castes were consuming the vast majority of reservation benefits, completely shutting out more historically degraded sub-groups (e.g., Valmikis and Mazhabi Sikhs in Punjab, Madigas in Andhra Pradesh).

**Core Legal Underpinnings:**

1. **Overruling Precedent**: This judgment explicitly overruled the 5-judge bench decision in *E.V. Chinnaiah v. State of Andhra Pradesh* (2004), which had erroneously held that the SC list under Article 341 forms a single, monolithic, homogeneous class that states cannot alter.

2. **Article 14 & 16(4) Interplay**: The majority ruled that the principle of "Sub-classification" is a facet of substantive equality. True equality requires treating unequals unequally within a class to prevent the exclusion of the weakest. However, the court placed a rider: states cannot reserve 100% of a quota for a single sub-class and must base sub-classification on quantifiable, empirical data of under-representation.

**CLAT Passage Strategy:**
- The key doctrinal distinction is between **formal equality** (same treatment for all) and **substantive equality** (different treatment based on actual disadvantage).
- The passage will likely present a scenario where a sub-group within SC/ST claims under-representation, and the state wants to create a sub-quota.
- The correct answer will ALWAYS reference the need for **quantifiable, empirical data** — not just historical grievance or political demand.
- Remember: *E.V. Chinnaiah* was **overruled**. Do not select an answer based on Chinnaiah.

---

### H3. The Electoral Bonds Verdict (ADR v. Union of India) — Transparency vs. Privacy

**Key Timeline:**
- **February 15, 2024**: A 5-judge anonymous Constitution Bench unanimously struck down the scheme.

**Main Contextual Influence:** Introduced in the 2018 Finance Act, the scheme allowed unlimited, completely anonymous political donations by corporations. This created a massive asymmetry of information where the ruling party could access donor identities via state-owned banks (SBI), while the voting public remained entirely blind to potential quid pro quo corruption.

**Core Legal Underpinnings:**

1. **The Proportionality Test**: The Supreme Court applied the strict four-pronged Proportionality Test. It ruled that while curbing black money is a legitimate state aim, total donor anonymity is not the least restrictive means to achieve it.

2. **Infringement of Article 19(1)(a)**: The court established that the Right to Information is a vital structural subset of Freedom of Expression. For a voter to exercise their franchise effectively, information regarding who funds political parties is a constitutional prerequisite.

**CLAT Passage Strategy:**
- The passage will likely present a balance between two rights: (1) the right to privacy of donors vs. (2) the right to information of voters under Article 19(1)(a).
- The correct answer will **favor the voter's right to know** — because the Court held that information about political funding is essential for informed voting.
- The Court ordered SBI to disclose donor data. This became a major post-verdict controversy.
- If the question asks "what is the LEAST restrictive means to curb black money?" the answer will be about transparency mechanisms (e.g., real-time disclosure), not blanket anonymity.

### H4. Uttarakhand Uniform Civil Code (UCC) — Religious Autonomy vs. Gender Equality

**Key Timelines:**
- **February 7, 2024**: Passed by the Uttarakhand Legislative Assembly.
- **March 11, 2024**: Received Presidential Assent under Article 254(2) (necessary because succession and marriage sit on the Concurrent List, and this law overrode existing central personal laws).

**Main Contextual Influence:** The political push to fulfill Article 44 of the Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSPs), which urges the State to secure a uniform civil code for all citizens. The historical friction lay in balancing secular gender-equal laws with religious autonomy guaranteed under Articles 25 and 26.

**Core Legal Underpinnings:**

1. **The Tribal Conundrum**: To protect customs protected under Part XXI of the Constitution, the Code explicitly exempts Scheduled Tribes (under Article 366(25)). This created an immediate constitutional critique regarding whether a code can truly be "uniform" if it excludes specific demographic segments based on ethnicity.

2. **Privacy vs. State Regulation**: By criminalizing the non-registration of live-in relationships with up to 3 months of imprisonment, the law directly collided with the right to autonomy and spatial privacy established in *K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India* (2017).

**CLAT Passage Strategy:**
- The passage will likely present a conflict between Article 44 (DPSP — UCC) and Articles 25/26 (religious freedom), or Article 21 (privacy).
- The correct answer will often reference the **Shah Bano** principle: gender equality is a constitutional imperative, and personal laws cannot override it.
- The UCC's criminalization of live-in relationships is a high-yield topic for questions on privacy vs. state regulation.
- The fact that STs are exempt raises questions about whether the code is truly "uniform" — this is a classic trap answer. The correct answer should acknowledge that while imperfect, it is still a significant step toward Article 44.
