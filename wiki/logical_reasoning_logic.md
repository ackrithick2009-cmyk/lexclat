## 1. CLAT Logical Reasoning Format

CLAT tests **applied reasoning** — every question is grounded in a **passage or scenario**. The format post-2020 is entirely passage-based, eliminating standalone puzzles.

### Question Categories

| Type | Description | Example Trigger |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| **Argument Strength** | Is the argument strong or weak? | "Which option, if true, weakens this conclusion?" |
| **Assumption Identification** | What must be true for the argument to hold? | "The argument assumes which of the following?" |
| **Inference** | What can be logically drawn from the passage? | "Which of the following can be inferred?" |
| **Logical Sequence** | Correct ordering of events/statements | "Which of the following is the most logical conclusion?" |
| **Analogy** | Apply a rule from one situation to another | Legal principle application |

## 2. Arguments — Strengthening & Weakening
> *Incorporating the Unacademy LSAT-Style Framework.*

### The Core Framework (PAC Method)

An argument has:
- **Premise (P)**: The evidence, data, or facts offered (e.g., "Because X happened...")
- **Assumption (A)**: The invisible bridge. What the author believes but didn't state to get from Premise to Conclusion.
- **Conclusion (C)**: The main point the author wants you to accept (e.g., "Therefore, Y is true.")

**To STRENGTHEN**: Find an option that explicitly states the hidden assumption, supports a premise, or eliminates an alternative cause.  
**To WEAKEN**: Find an option that breaks the link between Premise and Conclusion by introducing a new, unaccounted variable (alternative cause).

### Legal Context Example

> **Argument**: "The government has proposed mandatory pre-trial detention for persons accused of organised crime. This is justified because organised crime syndicates have demonstrated an ability to intimidate witnesses when accused persons are on bail."
>
> **STRENGTHENS**: Studies show witness intimidation is 3x more likely in organised crime cases when accused persons have bail.
>
> **WEAKENS**: Courts have consistently held that pre-trial detention without individual risk assessment violates Article 21, and case-specific evaluations are equally effective.

### Trap Options to Avoid
- **Irrelevant true facts**: True but don't logically connect to the conclusion
- **Scope shifts**: Address a different issue than the argument
- **Extreme options**: Use words like "never," "always," "all" — often wrong

## 3. Assumptions & Conclusions

### Assumptions — The Negation Test
> If negating (making the opposite of) the option **destroys the argument**, it is a necessary assumption.

**Example:**
> *Argument*: "This law school produced the most Supreme Court judges; therefore, it has the best legal pedagogy."
>
> *Potential Assumption*: "Becoming a Supreme Court judge reflects the quality of legal training received."
>
> *Negate it*: "Becoming a Supreme Court judge does NOT reflect legal training quality." → This would destroy the argument. ✅ **Valid Assumption.**

### Conclusions — Must vs. Could
- A **valid conclusion** MUST follow from the premises — not just "could be true"
- Avoid options that go **beyond the evidence** in the passage (over-inference)

| Wrong Pattern | Why It's Wrong |
|--------------|----------------|
| "The Act is unconstitutional" when passage only says rights may be violated | Over-inference; passage doesn't confirm the court's verdict |
| "All lawyers are unethical" from one case | Overgeneralisation |

## 4. Analogical & Principle-Based Reasoning

### How It Works in CLAT
1. A **rule** or **legal principle** is stated
2. A **factual scenario** is presented
3. You must select which option is **most analogous** (similar in legally relevant ways)

### Key: Identify the Relevant Feature
The "analogous" case must share the **legally operative feature**, not just surface similarities.

**Example Principle**: *"A person who causes harm while performing an act with the consent of the victim cannot be held criminally liable, unless the act was done with the intent to cause grievous hurt."*

| Scenario | Applicable? | Reasoning |
|----------|------------|-----------|
| A doctor performs surgery; patient dies despite consent and no negligence | Yes ✅ | Consent present, no grievous hurt intent |
| A boxing match where one fighter seriously injures another intentionally | No ❌ | Intent to cause grievous hurt overrides consent defence |
| A tattoo artist causes infection unintentionally | Yes ✅ | Consent present; no intent to harm |

## 5. Legal Scenario Practice Sets

### Set A: Bail & Pre-Trial Detention

> **Passage**: "The Supreme Court has held that bail is the rule and jail is the exception in non-bailable offence cases. The state must demonstrate that the accused poses a flight risk, risk of evidence tampering, or threat to witnesses to justify pre-trial detention. Economic offences, however, have been treated as a special category where the gravity of the offence is itself a relevant consideration."

**Q1.** Rajan, a small-time trader, is accused of fraud involving ₹50,000. The prosecution argues he should be detained because the offence is an economic crime. Which of the following most strengthens Rajan's bail application?

- **(A)** Rajan has deep roots in the community and no prior criminal record
- **(B)** The prosecution has not shown that Rajan is a flight risk or poses a threat to witnesses
- **(C)** The amount involved is too small to be classified as a serious economic offence
- **(D)** Both (A) and (B)

**Answer: (D)** — The passage states the state must demonstrate specific risks; small economic value alone addresses the "gravity" exception from a different angle.

---

### Set B: Free Speech vs. Hate Speech

> **Passage**: "The right to free speech under Article 19(1)(a) is subject to reasonable restrictions under Article 19(2), including public order, decency, and incitement to offences. A restriction is 'reasonable' only if it is proportionate — the harm prevented must outweigh the right curtailed. Courts have held that speech which creates a 'clear and present danger' of imminent unlawful action may be restricted, but mere offensiveness or discomfort does not meet this threshold."

**Q2.** A comedian posts a satirical sketch mocking government policy. Some citizens claim it is offensive and file a complaint. The government blocks the video. Which conclusion is best supported by the passage?

- **(A)** The government's action is constitutional since it protects public order
- **(B)** Mere offensiveness does not justify restricting speech; the block is likely unconstitutional
- **(C)** The action is valid as Article 19(2) permits restrictions on indecent content
- **(D)** The comedian should have obtained government clearance before posting

**Answer: (B)** — The passage explicitly states "mere offensiveness or discomfort does not meet this threshold."

## 6. Logical Fallacies (High-Yield)

| Fallacy | Definition | Legal Example |
|---------|-----------|---------------|
| **Ad Hominem** | Attacking the person, not the argument | "His constitutional argument is flawed because he's a convicted criminal." |
| **False Dilemma** | Only two options presented when more exist | "Either we ban social media or accept misinformation." |
| **Slippery Slope** | One event will cause a chain of extreme consequences | "If we allow assisted dying, we'll allow killing healthy people." |
| **Appeal to Authority** | Citing authority as sole proof | "Eminent lawyers support this bill, so it must be constitutional." |
| **Circular Reasoning** | Conclusion restates the premise | "This law is valid because Parliament passed it, and Parliament only passes valid laws." |
| **Post Hoc** | After this, therefore because of this | "Crime rates fell after the policy — so the policy caused it." |
| **Hasty Generalisation** | Broad conclusion from insufficient evidence | "This one acquittal proves the justice system is broken." |

## 7. Official Practice Set: The Tyranny of Merit (CLAT 2024)

### Passage Extract
> "In a world where aspirations for upward mobility are fervent, the opportunities for achieving such dreams remain limited. When one generation falls short, the mantle of ambition passes to the next, embedding within it a heavy burden of responsibility. Failing to meet these expectations can lead to profound sorrow, and in the direst cases, even to suicide. It's in this landscape that coaching institutes assume a significant role, cultivating an atmosphere of uncertainty among students and parents. A stark discrepancy emerges between preparation for board examinations and competitive tests, amplifying the inequalities that plague the education system... Eminent thinker Michael J Sandel dubs this system the 'tyranny of merit', a sentiment echoed by the Supreme Court of India."

**Q104. Which of the following is not true regarding the education system as per the opinion of the author?**
(a) It is primarily based on the merit of the students.
(b) Merit of the students is not the determining factor.
(c) Examination plays a pivotal role in the education system.
(d) An individual's merit cannot be identified without examination.

**Q106. Which of the following is correct regarding the inequalities mentioned by the author?**
(a) There is no difference between the preparation of board and competitive examination.
(b) Education system accommodates the inequalities.
(c) Competitive examination leads to social equality.
(d) There are existing inequalities which are enlarged by the competitive examinations.

## 8. Official Practice Set: Words as Enigma (CLAT 2024)

### Passage Extract
> "Words remain an enigma, their presence within human sphere is a unique mystery. They form the intricate tapestry that convinces us of our existence, blurring lines between fact and fiction... Words cast darkness in the mind of the reader, absorb emotions, or stand as dry as a rock. Words can provoke smiles and elicit joy. They are like glow-worms momentarily dispelling the surrounding darkness and act as life-saving rafts amid the tumultuous ocean waves."

**Q85. What is the central theme of the passage?**
(a) The history of the human language
(b) The impact of words on human existence
(c) The importance of storytelling
(d) The mystery of human cognition

**Q89. Which of the following strengthens the author's idea of words being 'like glow-worms momentarily dispelling the surrounding darkness'?**
(a) Words help in understanding reality.
(b) Words illuminate the mind of the reader.
(c) Words help in escaping reality.
(d) Words cast darkness in the mind of the reader.
