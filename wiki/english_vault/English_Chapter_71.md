# English Vault: Chapter 71 – Words and Phrases of Foreign Origin

## 1. Concept Primer
Foreign words and phrases function as permanent loanwords, primarily derived from Latin, French, and Greek. They maintain their original linguistic structure while integrating into English. Mastery of these terms is essential for the CLAT examination, focusing on:
*   **Contextual Accuracy:** Ensuring the term fits the logical flow of a sentence.
*   **Denotative Precision:** Distinguishing between terms with similar appearances (e.g., *ad hoc* vs. *pro tem*).
*   **Register Awareness:** Utilizing these terms appropriately within formal legal or diplomatic frameworks.

## 2. Landmark Vocabulary Bank
The curriculum organizes 50 high-frequency terms into four distinct categories for systematic review:
*   **Latin Legalisms (Indices 1–10):** Vital for Legal Reasoning sections.
*   **Diplomatic/Political Terms (Indices 11–25):** Focus on essential phrases like *de facto* and *ex officio*.
*   **Sociological/Literary Terms (Indices 26–40):** Frequently tested in Reading Comprehension passages.
*   **Advanced Syntax (Indices 41–50):** Rare, high-weightage terminology.

## 3. Trap Analysis
Candidates must remain vigilant against common testing pitfalls:
*   **The "False Friend" Trap:** Avoid selecting literal synonyms; focus on the idiomatic equivalent of the foreign phrase (e.g., *faux pas*).
*   **The Contextual Shift:** An answer choice may be grammatically sound but contextually incorrect. The use of terms like *inter alia* in informal settings serves as a primary distractor.
*   **The Suffix/Prefix Confusion:** Be aware of gender and number agreements. Certain foreign adjectives must strictly align with the nouns they modify.
