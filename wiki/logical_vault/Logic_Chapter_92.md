# Chapter 92: Logical Reasoning (Foundation)

Logical Reasoning is a cornerstone of the CLAT examination, designed to test your ability to synthesize facts, identify patterns, and draw valid conclusions from provided premises.

---

## 1. The Nature of Logic in Law
In the context of legal entrance tests, logic is the mechanism used to correlate facts and statements through **Arguments**. 
*   **Validity over Truth:** Logic ensures a valid conclusion based *only* on the provided information. This conclusion may contradict common experience, but for the purpose of the exam, the provided statements are the absolute ground truth.
*   **No Deviation:** You must not use outside knowledge or personal "conscience" to influence the result.

## 2. Statement-Inferences
An inference is a logical consequence derived from given statements. 
*   **Direct Correlation:** A valid inference must follow directly from the provided text.
*   **Exclusion of Probability:** If an inference is only "possible" but not "certain," it is typically considered incorrect in formal logic unless specified otherwise.

### Example Case:
**Statements:**
1. All books are notebooks.
2. No notebook is pen.

**Correct Inference:** *No book is pen.*
**Reasoning:** Since all books are contained within the set of notebooks, and notebooks have zero overlap with pens, books must also have zero overlap with pens.

## 3. Syllogism (Aristotelian Logic)
Syllogism is a form of **mediate inference** where a conclusion is drawn from two or more premises.

### The Three Terms
Every valid syllogism consists of:
1.  **Major Term:** The predicate of the conclusion.
2.  **Minor Term:** The subject of the conclusion.
3.  **Middle Term:** Appears in both premises but disappears in the conclusion.

### Essential Rules for Validity
1.  **Distribution:** The middle term must be distributed (referenced in its entirety) at least once.
2.  **Negative Premises:**
    *   If one premise is negative, the conclusion **must** be negative.
    *   If both premises are negative, **no conclusion** can be drawn.
3.  **Particular Premises:**
    *   If one premise is particular (e.g., "Some..."), the conclusion must be particular.
    *   If both are particular, **no conclusion** can be drawn.

## 4. Statement-Assumptions
An assumption is an **implicit** premise—something taken for granted that is necessary for the statement to hold true.

### Example Case:
**Statement:** *"If you join a gym, you will definitely lose weight."*
**Implicit Assumption:** Joining a gym is a effective method for weight loss/reducing obesity.

---

## ⚖️ Strategic Note for CLAT
In **Critical Reasoning** passages, identifying the "Implicit Assumption" is often the key to weakening or strengthening an author's argument. Always ask: *"What must be true for this statement to make sense?"*
